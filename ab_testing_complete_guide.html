Confidence Intervals · Part 1
Why a single number
is never enough
Before we learn what a confidence interval is, let's understand why we even need one. This is the most important thing to understand before any formula.
The Problem: You Can't Measure Everyone

Imagine Netflix wants to know: "What is the average time our users spend watching per day?"

Netflix has 260 million subscribers worldwide. Can they ask every single person? No. It would take too long, cost too much, and many people wouldn't respond.

So what do they do? They take a sample — maybe 10,000 randomly chosen users. They measure watch time for those 10,000 people. They calculate the average.

Let's say the average comes out to 2.1 hours per day.

🍕 Analogy — Tasting the Soup

Imagine you're cooking a giant pot of soup for 500 people. You can't taste every drop — but you take a spoonful and taste it. That spoonful is your sample. The taste of that spoonful is your estimate of what the whole pot tastes like.

But here's the thing: if you took a different spoonful from a different part of the pot, you'd get a slightly different taste. Maybe a bit saltier. Maybe a bit blander. This variation is normal — and it's exactly the problem confidence intervals solve.

The Core Problem: Sample Results Vary

Here's what happens if Netflix ran this study 5 different times with 5 different random samples of 10,000 users:

Sample 1
2.1 hrs

n = 10,000 users

Sample 2
2.3 hrs

n = 10,000 users

Sample 3
1.9 hrs

n = 10,000 users

Sample 4
2.2 hrs

n = 10,000 users

Every sample gives a slightly different answer. So which one is the "real" average? The truth is: we don't know exactly. The true population mean is somewhere nearby — but our single number (called a point estimate) doesn't tell us how confident we should be, or how far off we might be.

⚠️ The Problem with Point Estimates

Saying "average watch time is 2.1 hours" is like saying "I'll arrive at 3pm" without mentioning that traffic could make it anywhere from 2:45pm to 3:30pm. The single number is useful, but dangerously incomplete without context about its uncertainty.

The Solution: Give a Range, Not Just a Number

Instead of reporting just one number, data scientists report a range of plausible values. This range is called a confidence interval (CI).

❌ Point Estimate Only
2.1 hours

One number. No uncertainty. You have no idea if this is a reliable estimate or a lucky coincidence from an unrepresentative sample. Dangerous to act on.

✅ Confidence Interval
1.98 – 2.22 hours

A range. Tells you the estimate is 2.1 hours, and you're 95% confident the true answer falls somewhere between 1.98 and 2.22. You can make much better decisions with this.

The confidence interval gives you a range around your point estimate.
Wider = more uncertainty. Narrower = more precision.
Confidence Intervals · Part 2
What a confidence
interval actually means
This is the most misunderstood concept in all of statistics. Even professional data scientists get this wrong. Let's get it right.
The Precise Definition — In Plain English

"A 95% confidence interval means: if we repeated our sampling process 100 times and built a confidence interval each time, about 95 of those 100 intervals would contain the true population value."

— The correct interpretation, always

Let's unpack this slowly, because it's tricky.

When we say "95% confidence," we're NOT saying "there's a 95% chance the true value is in this one interval." We're saying the method we used to build this interval is reliable 95% of the time.

Think of it like a fishing net. If your net catches fish 95% of the time you cast it, and you cast it once today and catch something — you don't know for certain if today was one of the lucky 95% or the unlucky 5%. But you trust the net because it works 95% of the time.

🎯 Analogy — The Horseshoe Game

Imagine you're playing horseshoes. You're not a perfect player — sometimes you land closer to the stake, sometimes further. But you're a consistent player: 95% of your throws land within 2 feet of the stake.

Each throw is like one sample, and "landing within 2 feet" is like building a CI that contains the true mean. The true stake doesn't move — but where your horseshoe lands varies. A 95% CI means your "horseshoe" (your interval) captures the stake 95% of the time.

Visualizing It: 100 Confidence Intervals

The best way to understand this is to see it. Below, we simulated taking 60 different random samples from the same population (true mean = 50). For each sample, we built a 95% CI. Look at what happens:

Notice: about 3 red ones — that's the expected ~5% miss rate.
⚠️ The #1 Misconception — Interviewers Test This Directly

WRONG: "There's a 95% probability that the true mean is between 1.98 and 2.22 hours."

WHY IT'S WRONG: The true mean is a fixed, unchanging number. It doesn't have a probability — it either IS between 1.98 and 2.22, or it ISN'T. We just don't know which. There's no probability involved for a fixed quantity.

RIGHT: "The process I used to build this interval captures the true mean 95% of the time. So I'm 95% confident in my method."

✅ Memory Trick

The 95% refers to the procedure, not this specific interval. Think of it as saying "I used a method that works 95% of the time." The interval itself either works or it doesn't — we just can't know which.

The Link to Hypothesis Testing

Confidence intervals and hypothesis tests are two different ways of answering the same question. They're deeply connected:

// The golden rule — works every time
If 0 is NOT inside your 95% CI for the difference → reject H₀ at α = 0.05
If 0 IS inside your 95% CI for the difference → fail to reject H₀ at α = 0.05

// Example: Testing if a new button color increases clicks
CI for (new clicks% − old clicks%) = [0.8%, 3.2%]
0 is not in this interval → statistically significant improvement ✓

This is extremely useful in interviews. If asked "is this result significant?", you can answer with either a p-value OR a confidence interval — both are valid, and mentioning both makes you look very rigorous.

Confidence Intervals · Part 3
Building a confidence
interval from scratch
Now we'll learn the formula. But we'll build up to it piece by piece — no formula until you understand what every part means.
The Building Blocks

A confidence interval has exactly 3 ingredients. Let's understand each one before combining them.

1
Your Best Guess (Point Estimate = x̄ or p̂)

This is simply the average you calculated from your sample. For means, it's called x̄ (x-bar). For proportions (like click rates), it's called p̂ (p-hat). This is the CENTER of your confidence interval.

📺 Netflix example: You sampled 10,000 users and found 24% clicked on a new thumbnail. So p̂ = 0.24. This is your best single guess for the true click rate.
2
How Spread Out Your Estimates Are (Standard Error = SE)

If you took many different samples of the same size, each would give a slightly different mean. The Standard Error measures how much these sample means typically vary. It's not the same as standard deviation — it measures the variability of the mean itself, not individual data points.

For a proportion: SE = √(p̂ × (1 − p̂) / n)
For a mean: SE = σ / √n

Key insight: SE shrinks as n grows. More data = estimates that vary less = smaller SE.
3
How Confident You Want to Be (Critical Value = z*)

How wide do you want your net? If you want to be 95% confident, you use z* = 1.96. If you want 99% confidence, you use z* = 2.576. Higher confidence = wider interval. You're trading precision for certainty.

90% confidence → z* = 1.645
95% confidence → z* = 1.96 (industry standard)
99% confidence → z* = 2.576
The Formula — Now It Makes Sense
// General formula for any confidence interval
CI = Point Estimate ± (Critical Value × Standard Error)

// For a mean (when you know population std dev σ)
CI = x̄ ± z* × (σ / √n)

// For a proportion (conversion rates, click rates, churn %)
CI = p̂ ± z* × √( p̂(1−p̂) / n )

The "±" means you go that amount in both directions from your estimate. The result is a lower bound and an upper bound — your interval.

Full Worked Example — Step by Step
🎬 Netflix — New Thumbnail Test

Netflix tests a new thumbnail design for a popular show. They show it to 500 random users. 120 of them click on it. What is the 95% confidence interval for the true click-through rate?

1
Calculate the Point Estimate (p̂)

p̂ = clicks / total users = 120 / 500 = 0.24 (24%)

This is our best guess: 24% of users click the thumbnail.

2
Calculate the Standard Error (SE)

SE = √(p̂ × (1 − p̂) / n) = √(0.24 × 0.76 / 500) = √(0.0003648) = 0.0191

This tells us: sample proportions typically vary by about ±1.91% from the true rate.

3
Choose the Critical Value (z* for 95%)

We want 95% confidence, so z* = 1.96

This is the z-score that captures the middle 95% of the standard normal distribution.

4
Build the Interval

Margin of Error = z* × SE = 1.96 × 0.0191 = 0.0374

Lower bound = 0.24 − 0.0374 = 20.3%

Upper bound = 0.24 + 0.0374 = 27.7%

Result: 95% CI = [20.3%, 27.7%]

What does this mean in plain English? "We're 95% confident that the true click rate for this thumbnail — across all Netflix users — is somewhere between 20.3% and 27.7%."
The 95% CI for Netflix thumbnail click rate: our estimate of 24% sits in the center, with uncertainty extending to either side.
🎯 How to Use This Result

If the old thumbnail had a 19% click rate, and 19% is BELOW our confidence interval lower bound of 20.3%, we can say the new thumbnail is statistically significantly better at 95% confidence.

If the old thumbnail had a 21% click rate, and 21% is INSIDE our interval [20.3%, 27.7%], we cannot conclude the new thumbnail is better — the difference might just be random variation.

Confidence Intervals · Part 4
What makes an interval
wider or narrower?
Understanding the three levers that control CI width is crucial — it directly impacts how you design experiments in the real world.
The Three Levers

Look at the CI formula: CI = p̂ ± z* × √(p̂(1−p̂) / n)

There are exactly three things that affect the width of a confidence interval:

Lever 1
Sample Size (n) ↑

More data = narrower interval. Because with more data, your sample mean is a more reliable estimate. More samples = less randomness.

Width ∝ 1/√n → NARROWER ✓
Lever 2
Confidence Level ↑

Want to be more certain? You have to cast a wider net. Going from 95% to 99% confidence means your interval gets noticeably wider.

95%→99% confidence → WIDER ↑
Lever 3
Data Variability (σ) ↑

Noisier data = wider interval. If users' watch times vary wildly (some watch 30 min, some watch 8 hours), your estimate is less reliable.

Higher variance → WIDER ↑

A/B Testing · Part 1
What is A/B Testing,
really?
Every major tech company lives and dies by A/B testing. Let's understand it from first principles — no jargon, just the core idea.
The Core Problem A/B Testing Solves

Imagine you work at Spotify. Your team changes the color of the "Play" button from green to blue. The next week, users play 8% more songs. Was it the button color? Or was it because a famous artist released an album that week? Or because it was the weekend?

⚠️ The Big Problem: Correlation vs Causation

Without a controlled experiment, you cannot know if your change caused the improvement. Maybe engagement goes up every spring. Maybe a competitor went down. Maybe it's just random variation. You can observe that two things happened together (correlation), but you can't prove one caused the other.

A/B testing solves this by running both versions simultaneously on randomly split user groups. Because both groups are exposed at the same time under the same conditions, the ONLY systematic difference between them is the thing you changed.

🌱 Analogy — The Farmer's Experiment

A farmer wants to know if a new fertilizer actually helps plants grow. He could use the new fertilizer this year and compare to last year's harvest. But what if this year had more rain? More sun? Different pests? He can't know if it was the fertilizer or the weather.

The right approach: take one field, divide it in half. Use the new fertilizer on the left half, old fertilizer on the right half. Same weather. Same soil. Same time. Now if the left half grows better, it was definitely the fertilizer. That's an A/B test.

🅰️ Group A — Control

Sees the current, unchanged version of your product. This is your baseline. Whatever their behavior is, that's what you compare against.

Think of this as the "do nothing" option. If you never ran this experiment, all users would behave like Group A.

🅱️ Group B — Treatment

Sees the new version with your one change. If their behavior differs from Group A's in a statistically significant way, your change caused it.

This is the "what if" option. Group B shows you what would happen if you shipped the change to everyone.

💡 Why Random Assignment is Critical

You must assign users to groups randomly. If you put all iPhone users in Group B and Android users in Group A, any difference you measure might be about phone type, not your change. Random assignment ensures that on average, both groups are identical in every way except what you're testing.

Real Examples from Major Companies
📧 Obama Campaign (2008)

Tested different email subject lines for fundraising. One subject line outperformed others by 40% in donations. A/B testing helped raise an estimated $60 million in additional donations. This is probably the most famous A/B test in political history.

🔴 Netflix

Tests different thumbnail images for shows and movies. The same show can have dozens of different thumbnails simultaneously shown to different user groups. Netflix attributes significant engagement improvements to their aggressive thumbnail testing program.

🔍 Google

Once tested 41 different shades of blue for their toolbar links to find which shade generated the most clicks. They run an estimated 10,000+ A/B tests per year. Almost every change to Google Search has been A/B tested.

📦 Amazon

Tests everything from button placement to product description formats to checkout flows. Jeff Bezos famously said A/B testing is one of the most important drivers of Amazon's product improvement process.

A/B Testing · Part 2
Setting up an A/B test
the right way
Before you run a single experiment, you need to define several things clearly. Poorly defined experiments give useless — or worse, misleading — results.
Step 1 — Define Your Hypothesis (Before Looking at Any Data)

Every A/B test starts with a clear, written-down hypothesis. This isn't optional. If you define your hypothesis after seeing the data, you're cheating — you'll unconsciously choose the hypothesis that the data already supports.

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

The "skeptical" baseline. Assumes your change had no effect. "The new button color produces the same click rate as the old one."

Alternative Hypothesis (H₁)

What you're trying to prove. "The new button color produces a different (or higher) click rate than the old one."

⚖️ Analogy — The Court of Law

Think of it like a criminal trial. The null hypothesis is "innocent until proven guilty." You need strong evidence (low p-value) to reject the null and conclude something changed. The burden of proof is on the data.

Just like a jury doesn't say "definitely innocent" when they acquit — they say "not enough evidence to convict" — in statistics, you never "accept" H₀, you just "fail to reject" it.

// Typical A/B test hypothesis setup
H₀: p_treatment = p_control → no difference in conversion rate
H₁: p_treatment ≠ p_control → some difference exists (two-tailed)
OR
H₁: p_treatment > p_control → treatment is specifically better (one-tailed)

// Use two-tailed when direction is unknown, one-tailed when you
// specifically predict improvement (and you must commit before the test)
Step 2 — Choose Your Metric Carefully

Not all metrics are created equal. Choosing the wrong metric can make a harmful change look beneficial. You need three types of metrics for every serious experiment:

Metric TypeWhat It IsExampleRole in Decision
Primary The single most important thing you're trying to improve. Your north star for this test. Conversion rate, revenue per user, day-7 retention This decides go / no-go. Pre-specify ONE primary metric.
Guardrail Metrics that should NOT get worse. Safety checks to prevent you from shipping something harmful. App crash rate, support ticket volume, unsubscribe rate If any guardrail fails, don't ship — even if primary looks great.
Secondary Additional metrics that help you understand why the primary metric moved. Time on page, scroll depth, feature adoption rate Diagnostic only. Don't use for the final go/no-go call.
⚠️ Real Scenario: When Primary Wins But Guardrail Fails

What happened: A team tests an aggressive push notification strategy. Primary metric (DAU) goes up 4%. Great! But support tickets for "too many notifications" triple. Unsubscribe rate doubles.

What should happen: Don't ship. Short-term engagement gains are not worth destroying the user relationship. The guardrail metrics caught a serious problem the primary metric missed.

Step 3 — Ensure Groups Are Truly Random and Equal

The whole validity of an A/B test rests on one assumption: the two groups are comparable except for the thing you're testing. Randomization is how you achieve this.

Things that must be balanced between groups:

  • Demographics (age, gender, location)
  • Device type (mobile vs desktop)
  • Account age (new vs veteran users)
  • Usage patterns (heavy vs light users)
  • Time of sign-up within the experiment

With true random assignment and large enough samples, the law of large numbers ensures these balance out automatically — no manual matching needed.

🚨 What Can Go Wrong: SRM

Sample Ratio Mismatch (SRM) happens when your actual split (say 47%/53%) differs from your planned split (50%/50%). This suggests a bug in your randomization — maybe the new version crashes on Android and those users bail out before being counted.

How to detect: Run a chi-square test on the actual vs expected group sizes. If p < 0.05, you have SRM — stop, don't analyze results, fix the bug first.

A/B Testing · Part 3
How many users
do you actually need?
One of the most common interview questions. Running an underpowered test is like flipping a coin twice and concluding it's fair — not enough data to know anything reliable.
The Problem With Too Little Data

Imagine you flip a coin 4 times and get 3 heads. Does that mean the coin is biased? Maybe — but with only 4 flips, you don't have enough data to say anything confidently. You'd need many more flips to know for sure.

The same principle applies to A/B tests. If you test on 50 users per group, even a real improvement might not show up as statistically significant — simply because you didn't have enough data to detect it.

⚠️ The Two Types of "Wrong" in Underpowered Tests

Type I Error (False Positive): You declare a winner when there's actually no real difference. You ship a change that does nothing — or hurts users.

Type II Error (False Negative): You declare "no difference" when there actually IS a real improvement. You throw away a feature that would have helped users, just because your test didn't have enough data to detect the improvement.

Power Analysis — Calculating Sample Size Before the Test
Statistical Power

The probability that your test will correctly detect a real effect when it exists. Standard industry requirement: 80% power. This means if there's a real difference, your test has an 80% chance of finding it.

Before running any A/B test, you need to decide on four things:

1
Significance Level (α) — How Often Can You Be Wrong?

Standard: α = 0.05. This means you accept a 5% chance of incorrectly declaring a winner when nothing actually changed. Lower α = stricter = fewer false positives = needs more data.

2
Power (1−β) — How Sensitive Is Your Test?

Standard: 80% power. This means you have an 80% chance of detecting a real effect. Higher power = fewer missed improvements = needs more data.

3
Baseline Metric — What's the Current Rate?

What does your metric look like today, before any change? e.g., current conversion rate = 10%. You get this from historical data.

4
Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE) — What Change Actually Matters?

This is the most important and most overlooked input. You need to decide: "What is the smallest improvement that would be worth shipping?"

If conversion is 10% and you'd only ship for a 2%+ lift, your MDE = 2 percentage points (not 2% of 10% — be careful here). Smaller MDE means you're trying to detect subtler changes, which requires much more data.
// Sample size formula for comparing two proportions
n ≈ 2 × (z_{α/2} + z_β)² × p̄(1−p̄) / δ²

z_{α/2} = 1.96 for α=0.05 (critical value for significance)
z_β = 0.84 for 80% power
p̄ = average of control and treatment proportions
δ = minimum detectable effect (the difference you care about)

// Result: n is the number needed PER GROUP (multiply by 2 for total)

How Long Should You Run the Test?

You have your sample size target. Now — how many days do you run the experiment? It's not just "run until you hit n." There are three things to think about:

Business Cycles

Always run for at least 1–2 full weeks. User behavior on Monday is different from Saturday. You need to capture full weekly patterns. A test run only on weekdays will give biased results.

Novelty Effect

When users first see something new, they interact with it out of pure curiosity — then engagement drops back to normal. If you stop the test too early (before this "novelty bump" fades), you'll overestimate the improvement.

Never Peek Early

Looking at results mid-experiment and stopping when you see p < 0.05 is called "peeking" — and it's a major statistical sin. Your false positive rate can jump from 5% to 25%+ if you check frequently.

A/B Testing · Part 4
Reading and interpreting
your results
The experiment is done. Now what? Here's exactly how to analyze results and — most importantly — what NOT to conclude.
The Z-Test for Two Proportions

The most common A/B test compares two proportions — conversion rates, click rates, retention rates. After the experiment, you use a Z-test to determine if the difference between the two groups is statistically significant or just random noise.

// Step 1: Calculate the pooled proportion
p̂_pooled = (successes_A + successes_B) / (n_A + n_B)

// Step 2: Calculate the standard error of the difference
SE = √[ p̂_pooled × (1 − p̂_pooled) × (1/n_A + 1/n_B) ]

// Step 3: Calculate the Z-score
Z = (p̂_B − p̂_A) / SE

// Step 4: Decision
|Z| > 1.96 → reject H₀ → statistically significant result
|Z| ≤ 1.96 → fail to reject H₀ → no significant difference detected
💡 Why Pooled Proportion?

We pool the proportions from both groups because we're testing under the assumption that H₀ is true — meaning both groups come from the same population. Combining them gives us a better (more precise) estimate of the common "true" proportion under H₀.

The P-Value — Plain English Explanation

After running the Z-test, you get a p-value. This is the single most misunderstood number in all of statistics. Let's nail it.

"The p-value is the probability of seeing a difference as large as yours (or larger), assuming there was actually no real difference at all."

In other words: How surprising is your data, if we assumed H₀ was true?

A small p-value means: "This data would be really rare if nothing had actually changed — so something probably did change."

A large p-value means: "This data is pretty normal even if nothing changed — so we can't rule out random chance."

❌ WRONG Interpretation

"The p-value is the probability that H₀ is true."

"A p-value of 0.03 means there's a 3% chance the null hypothesis is correct."

This is wrong — and interviewers specifically probe for this mistake.

vs
✅ CORRECT Interpretation

"The p-value is the probability of observing this result (or more extreme) IF the null hypothesis were true."

"A p-value of 0.03 means: if there were truly no effect, we'd only see a difference this large 3% of the time by random chance."

The p-value is the area in the tail(s) beyond your Z-score.
Shaded red area = p-value. When this is small (<0.05), we reject H₀.
Statistical Significance vs Practical Significance

This distinction is crucial and often overlooked. A result can be statistically significant but practically meaningless — and vice versa.

📊 Statistically Significant but NOT Practically Significant

You test a new recommendation algorithm on 10 million users. Conversion rate improves from 12.000% to 12.001%. With 10M users, this is statistically significant (tiny p-value). But is a 0.001% improvement worth re-engineering your entire recommendation system? Almost certainly not.

Large sample sizes can make even trivial differences "significant."

📊 Practically Significant but NOT Statistically Significant

You test a new checkout flow on 30 users per group. Conversion goes from 10% to 25% — a massive 15 percentage point improvement! But with only 30 users, this does not reach statistical significance. With more data, it almost certainly would.

Small sample sizes can hide even real, large effects.

🎯 Interview Answer Template

When asked "is this result significant?", always address BOTH:

1. Statistical significance: "The p-value is 0.02, which is below our threshold of α=0.05, so the result is statistically significant."

2. Practical significance: "The effect size is a 3.2 percentage point improvement in conversion, which exceeds our MDE of 2pp and translates to approximately $2.4M additional annual revenue."

Answering both makes you sound like someone who has actually shipped products, not just passed statistics exams.

A/B Testing · Part 5
The pitfalls that sink
even experienced teams
These are the mistakes that produce bad decisions even when the statistics look right. Knowing these makes you look like someone with real field experience.
1 The Peeking Problem

You launch a test. Three days in, you check results. You see p = 0.04. Exciting! You stop the test and declare a winner.

This is one of the most common — and most damaging — mistakes in A/B testing.

P-value over time for a test where the TRUE effect is zero (no difference exists).
Notice how it dips below 0.05 multiple times due to random chance before stabilizing.

When you repeatedly check a running test and stop it as soon as you see significance, your actual false positive rate explodes:

How often you peekYour actual false positive rate
Check once (at the end)5% (as intended)
Check twice8.3%
Check 5 times14.2%
Check every day for 20 days25%+
✅ The Fix

Commit to your sample size before the test starts. Don't look at significance values mid-experiment. If you absolutely need interim looks (for very long tests), use Sequential Testing methods like the alpha-spending function, which are designed for this purpose.

2 Multiple Testing Problem

Your A/B test tracks 20 different metrics. At α=0.05, even if your change did absolutely nothing, you'd expect to see 1 metric appear significant purely by chance. If you then say "look, metric #14 improved!", you're fooling yourself.

// Probability of at least one false positive in m tests
P(false positive) = 1 − (1 − α)^m

m=1: 1 − 0.95^1 = 5.0% ← intended
m=5: 1 − 0.95^5 = 22.6% ← 4× higher than intended!
m=10: 1 − 0.95^10 = 40.1% ← nearly coin flip
m=20: 1 − 0.95^20 = 64.2% ← you'll almost certainly find something

The Bonferroni Correction: If you must test m metrics, use α* = α/m as your threshold. Testing 20 metrics at α=0.05? Use α* = 0.05/20 = 0.0025 as your significance threshold instead. This keeps your overall false positive rate at 5%.

🎯 Best Practice

Pre-specify exactly ONE primary metric before the test. Use other metrics only for understanding (diagnosis), not for the final go/no-go decision. This eliminates the multiple testing problem entirely for your main decision.

3 Network Effects & User Interference

Standard A/B tests assume users in Group A and users in Group B don't interact with each other. This is called the "Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption" (SUTVA). On many platforms, this assumption breaks down.

🚗 Uber Example

You test a new driver incentive on 50% of drivers (Group B). Group B drivers work more hours to earn the incentive. This means fewer rides are available for Group A drivers, who earn less. Group A's behavior changed — not because of any treatment, but because Group B's behavior affected them. Your control group is contaminated.

👥 Facebook Example

You test a new notification for 50% of users. Those users (Group B) interact more with Group A users. Group A users respond more because they're receiving more interactions. Group A's engagement goes up — not because of any treatment — making your treatment look less effective than it actually is (or your control group better than it actually is).

💡 Solution: Geo-Based or Cluster Randomization

Instead of randomly assigning individual users, randomize by geographic regions or social clusters. For example, Uber tests by city: drivers in Chicago get treatment, drivers in Denver are control. This way, the groups don't interfere with each other because they're geographically separated.

4 Simpson's Paradox

This one is mind-bending. Simpson's Paradox is when a trend that appears in your aggregate results reverses when you look at subgroups. This can make a harmful treatment look beneficial at first glance.

Treatment appears to win overall (green bar higher), but loses in BOTH subgroups (mobile and desktop).
This is Simpson's Paradox — the aggregate hides the truth.
💊 Classic Real-World Example — UC Berkeley Admissions (1973)

At first glance, UC Berkeley's data showed that men were admitted at a higher rate than women (44% vs 35%) — suggesting gender bias against women. But when researchers looked at individual departments, women were actually admitted at slightly higher rates than men in most departments.

What happened? Women were applying to more competitive departments with lower overall acceptance rates. The aggregated number hid the opposite truth. This is Simpson's Paradox at work.

⚠️ Always Segment Your Results

After any A/B test, don't just look at aggregate results. Break down by key segments: mobile vs desktop, new vs returning users, geographic regions, age groups. A "winning" change overall might be actively hurting an important user segment.

5 Survivorship Bias & Novelty Effect

Novelty Effect: When users see something new, they interact with it out of curiosity. Engagement spikes temporarily — then settles back to its real long-term level. If you stop the test during the spike, you overestimate the long-term benefit.

Fix: Run the test long enough for the novelty to wear off. Monitor how the effect evolves over time. A feature that looks great in week 1 but flat in week 3 might just be a novelty, not a genuine improvement.

Survivorship Bias: If users who have a bad experience leave your product entirely, you might not see their negative signal — they're gone from your dataset. The users remaining in your test might be a self-selected "survivor" group.

Example: A new, complex feature drives away casual users. The remaining users (who stayed) engage more — but your total user base shrinks. Looking only at engagement of remaining users shows a false positive.

The Business Problem
🐦 Twitter / X — Onboarding Team

Twitter's growth team notices that most users who sign up never come back after day 1. Step 1 — Define Hypotheses and Metrics
H₀: p_new = p_old = 0.30
→ simplified onboarding has no effect

H₁: p_new > p_old
→ simplified onboarding improves retention

α = 0.05, Power = 80%, MDE = 2pp

We chose a one-tailed test here because we specifically believe the new flow will improve retention — not just change it. We committed to this direction before the test.

Metrics defined before the test:

  • Primary: Day-7 retention rate
  • Guardrail: Time-to-first-tweet (shouldn't increase), support tickets ("can't complete onboarding"), app crash rate
  • Secondary: Day-1 retention, number of accounts followed in first session, email confirmation rate
Step 2 — Calculate Required Sample Size
// Known values
p_control = 0.30 → current retention rate
p_treatment = 0.32 → target after +2pp MDE
p̄ = (0.30 + 0.32)/2 = 0.31
z_{α/2} = 1.645 → one-tailed test at α=0.05
z_β = 0.84 → 80% power
δ = 0.02 → MDE = 2 percentage points

// Sample size calculation
n = 2 × (1.645 + 0.84)² × 0.31 × 0.69 / (0.02)²
= 2 × (2.485)² × 0.2139 / 0.0004
= 2 × 6.175 × 0.2139 / 0.0004
= 6,611 users per group

With 50,000 new users per day and a 50/50 split, we get 25,000 per group per day. We need 6,611 per group — less than 1 day of data technically. But we should still run for 14 days minimum (2 full weeks) to account for day-of-week effects and novelty.
